## 1. Executive Summary

**The business.** A premium, strictly non-medical boutique concierge for relatively independent elderly residents of Taipei — sold to their time-poor or overseas adult children who are buying peace of mind. Services: companionship, medical-appointment accompaniment and family liaison, technology coaching and scam defense, admin/paperwork, social outings, and a design-led digital legacy and tech organization line. It occupies the open middle between cheap commodity care brokers and heavy clinical live-in care, competing on reliability, cultural fluency, and beautifully designed family communication — not on price.

**Why now.** Taiwan became a *super-aged society* at the end of 2025 (20.06% aged 65+), and **Taipei City has the highest concentration in the country at 24.18% — nearly one in four residents.** Public Long-Term Care (LTC 3.0, launched Sept 2025) is disability-/means-tested and covers medical and ADL needs only; independent "young-old" seniors are unserved by it.

**The founder's edge.** As a Taiwanese native, Félicia clears the single biggest barrier in local senior services — trust and cultural/linguistic fluency. At 37 she is eligible for the citizen-only **Young Entrepreneur Loan**. Her design background is a moat, not a side skill: a trustworthy brand in a generic sector, a signature **UX family report** showing visit outcomes, and a scarce **digital legacy & senior interface optimization** service line.

**Financials (corrected, realistic).**
- Hard startup: ~NT$190,000 (€5,600) — well under the ceiling, leaving a reserve.
- Premium pricing: NT$700/h (4-hour minimum for one-off bookings; flexible micro-visits for subscribers) + monthly retainers NT$10,000–18,000 + projects NT$15,000–35,000.
- **Mature solo gross: ~NT$91,000–110,000/month; net ~NT$65,000–85,000/month (€1,900–2,500)** — strong vs. Taipei median net (≈NT$45,000–55,000).
- Break-even: month 4–6. Scaling funded by the Young Entrepreneur Loan.

## 2. Market & Competitor Analysis

**Macro.** ~4.67 million Taiwanese were 65+ at end-2025 against just 2.68 million under-15s; births hit a record low for a tenth straight year. Taiwan went from "aged" to "super-aged" in ~8 years, faster than Japan, Korea, or the U.S. Taipei is the epicenter at 24.18%.

**The buyer is the adult child (B2B2C).** Delivered to the senior; chosen, audited, and paid for by the 35–55 "sandwich generation" or overseas Taiwanese. Strong filial-piety norms create the obligation; time and distance create the need. They don't buy commodity care — they buy their parent's dignity and their own relief.

**The public system leaves the premium layer open.** LTC 3.0 is excellent but targets functional disability/dementia (grades 2–8 via the 1966 hotline) and is chronically underfunded (~0.26% of GDP vs. OECD 1.7%). Independent seniors don't qualify, don't want the label, or exhaust their quota — and self-pay.

| Competitor tier | Scope | Price | Why it loses the premium family |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Public LTC 3.0** | Severe disability / dementia | Heavily subsidized | Means-tested, rigid itemized limits; autonomous seniors unserved |
| **Migrant live-in caregivers** (~228k nationally; ~36,940 Taipei City) | Constant ADL assistance | NT$26,000–35,000/mo | Requires Barthel-scale disability proof; full-time live-in; language gaps; no lifestyle engagement or family reporting |
| **Hourly broker platforms** (UCarer 優照護, Angel 家天使, Home心) | On-demand basic companion / medical sitting | NT$250–500/h | High worker churn, broker-assigned matching, no brand or specialized reporting |
| **Félicia — premium lifestyle concierge** | Active lifestyle support, digital safety, interface coaching, digital organization | **NT$700/h** | Bespoke consistency, design-led reporting, native fluency, disciplined non-medical boundaries |

## 3. Position, Services & Pricing

Positioning: *"The concierge you'd want for your own parents."* Premium, vetted, native, English-capable for overseas families.

### A. À-la-carte hourly concierge — NT$700/h
- **Medical-appointment mastery:** scheduling, premium transit, sitting in on consultations, then translating the doctor's instructions into a clear written summary for the family.
- **Digital defense & tech coaching:** video calls, mobile banking/grocery apps, and a high-demand **scam-prevention** curriculum based on identifying deceptive interface designs and phishing patterns.
- **Companionship & admin:** outings, prescription management, paperwork, subsidy navigation (including helping eligible clients claim the LTC 3.0 they're entitled to).
- **Minimum policy:** 4-hour minimum (NT$2,800) for **one-off/new** bookings — protects margin against Taipei transit friction. **Retainer subscribers get flexible shorter micro-visits** (e.g., a 90-minute scam check or 2-hour accompaniment) as a subscriber benefit, removing the friction of forcing every short task into a 4-hour block.

### B. Monthly "Peace of Mind" retainers — the recurring core
Sold to the adult child as a subscription for long-distance parental care. Retainer visit-hours are drawn from Félicia's billable capacity (they are **not** additive to it — see §6).
- **Silver — NT$10,000/mo:** ~3 visits/month, bi-weekly digital check-in, standard monthly report.
- **Gold — NT$18,000/mo:** ~6 visits/month, priority accompaniment booking, the signature **Design-Led UX Family Report**.
- **The moat — the UX Family Report:** a proprietary, secure, beautifully structured digital report (calm typography, visit logs, mood/engagement notes, home-safety status, clear next-month action items) instead of a messy text thread. Directly leverages Félicia's UI/UX design background to turn care reporting into a tangible, high-value corporate artifact.

### C. High-ticket projects — "Digital Legacy & Memory Preservation" — NT$15,000–35,000
A premium service to organize and safeguard a senior's digital footprint. Directly leverages her UI/UX design background. Services include:
- Consolidating decades of family photos and videos from old phones, digital cameras, and scattered SD cards into a single secure, shared cloud vault.
- Optimizing smartphone and tablet user interfaces to be highly legible and senior-friendly (reducing UI clutter, adjusting contrast, disabling deceptive ad patterns, and setting up simplified settings).
- Establishing secure, simplified password management and credential legacy plans.

## 4. Operations, Logistics & Risk Shielding

### 4.1 Premium transport policy
- **No senior is ever transported on a scooter.** All senior travel during sessions uses pre-booked taxis or premium ride-hailing, **billed directly to the client's card on file as a pass-through** (not a business cost, not margin).
- Félicia's own scooter is used only for solo admin errands in dry weather; in Taipei's rain seasons, default to transit/taxi to protect the premium image.

### 4.2 Legal onboarding (three documents)
1. **Non-medical co-responsibility agreement** — signed by the paying adult child, explicitly acknowledging the service is non-clinical. *Note: this clarifies and reduces liability; under Taiwanese law it does not fully eliminate it. Have a local lawyer draft it.*
2. **Emergency protocol form** — authorizes the concierge to call 119 and initiate hospital transit immediately in an emergency, before reaching the family.
3. **Financial boundary policy** — staff never handle cash, checkbooks, or bank passwords. Elder financial abuse is the sector's #1 reputational and legal risk, so all errands/reimbursements run through a centralized digital billing account (LINE Pay / ECPay 綠界 / NewebPay 藍新 — local rails, not Stripe).

### 4.3 Scope & certification
Complete the 照顧服務員 (care-attendant) single-level certification (~90-hour course + exam, often subsidized): cheap, and it builds trust with families and referral partners. **But hold a hard non-medical line** — no injections, wound care, tube feeding. Refer clinical needs to nurses/the public system, and make that discipline a brand feature.

### 4.4 The "Félicia Method" playbook (scaling without losing trust)
During Year 1, document the workflows — luxury tone-of-voice guide, the visual-reporting blueprint, the anti-scam verification checklist. Framing the offer as a *methodology* rather than a person is what lets her recruit, train, and certify staff under the brand in Year 2.

## 5. Marketing — two channels, native-first

The earlier overhaul proposed going wealth-only; that starves the pipeline. Run **both**, because they capture families at different moments:

**Channel 1 — Acute-need / volume (primary for cash flow).** Hospital **discharge-planning units** (出院準備服務), neighborhood clinics, pharmacies, rehab centers, community LTC stations. These meet anxious families *at the exact moment of need* — the highest-intent leads.

**Channel 2 — Wealth / positioning (premium pull).** Private wealth managers and family offices in Da'an/Xinyi, estate-planning and elder-law firms, and high-end clubs. Slower and gatekept, and timed to succession rather than daily care — so a *positioning* and high-ticket channel, not the volume engine.

**Native Taiwanese families are the core market.** Expat chambers (BCCT, CCIFT) and overseas-Taiwanese networks are a *secondary* segment, not the driver — her decisive advantage is native trust with ordinary affluent local families, not a foreigner niche.

**Content (for the 35–55 buyer, via Facebook/LINE/LinkedIn):** local senior-scam alerts; "The UX of Aging" — how custom senior UI configurations prevent scam clicks and reduce daily tech frustration; and infographics de-mystifying where LTC 3.0 stops and premium support begins.

## 8. Risks & Mitigations

| Risk | Mitigation |
|---|---|
| **Trust / safety** (alone with vulnerable people) | Certification, vetting, insurance, transparent visit logs and references |
| **Elder financial abuse exposure** | Financial-boundary policy: never handle cash/passwords; centralized digital billing |
| **Scope creep into clinical care** | Hard non-medical boundary; refer to nurses/public system; make discipline a feature |
| **Liability after an incident** | Three-document onboarding + comprehensive insurance + 119 protocol; lawyer-drafted (waivers reduce, don't eliminate, liability) |
| **Solo capacity ceiling (~NT$110k/mo)** | Plan the network model early; the loan funds it |
| **Commodity price pressure** | Never compete on hourly price; sell trust, communication, retainers |
| **Prepaid-contract regulation** (escrow rules tightened) | Monthly/post-paid retainers; no large prepaid blocks |
| **Slow ramp / seasonality** | Bridge income in Q1–Q2; lead with high-intent discharge-planning/clinic referrals |

## 9. 90-Day Launch Plan

**Days 1–30 — Legal & brand.** Register the LLC + tax registration. Enroll in the 90-hour care-attendant course + CPR. Build brand, secure booking page, and engineer the UX Family Report template. Bind insurance. Draft the three onboarding documents with a local lawyer.

**Days 31–60 — Pipeline.** Visit 15–20 acute-need partners (discharge planners, clinics, pharmacies, LTC stations) **and** seed 5–8 wealth/elder-law contacts in Da'an/Xinyi. Launch the adult-child content channel (scam prevention, UI customization). Land the **first 3 paying clients**.

**Days 61–90 — Convert & systematize.** Turn one-offs into retainers; deliver the first UX reports and capture testimonials; run one free community scam-awareness session; deliver the first digital legacy project; codify workflows into the Method playbook for Year-2 expansion.
